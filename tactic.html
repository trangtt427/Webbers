Tax Accounting for Digital Assets

Automated solutions to drastically reduce the time businesses spend on tax reporting for digital assets.

opportunity

Designing in Uncharted Territories

I joined Tactic in 2021 as a consulting designer (first designer and honorary Tactician #4) to help bring their crypto accounting platform to the market. Crypto was starting to appear on more company balance sheets these days, so that meant more regulations and guidelines around how to report income on them. Not too many digital asset accounting platforms were in the market yet, especially for companies that handle about 1,000+ transactions a month.

Companies that deal with crypto needed a more efficient and reliable approach to bookkeeping and reporting, otherwise they can get hit with hefty tax fines. I partnered with the lead product manager and in-house accountant to understand what closing the books—the end goal for all accountants—look like. I was eventually able to wrap my mind around complex accounting workflows like reconciling, chart of accounts, journaling, and crypto concepts like wallets and staking.

research

Learning About Taxes and Crypto

In the beginning, we relied heavily on what other companies were doing (mainly Cryptio, Koinly, and CryptoTaxCalculator) and identified the problems they were solving—and for what types of customers. We were constrained with resources, but we did have our own accountant as well as a few customer friendlies to learn from. I held a few Q+A sessions to better understand what accountants and finance stakeholders do ahead of tax season. The workstreams varied depending on the person, but the goal remained the same: manage finances in a clean and compliant way.

interesting insight

The crypto accounting process was very labor-intensive, and took one of our customers up to 40 hours a month.

One user, a Head of Finance, had his engineers pulling transactions across different wallets and exchanges to manually calculate their cost-basis and fees. This tedious workflow took away valuable development time from engineers.

solutions

The Transactions Page

It was clear that users needed all of their crypto transactions visible in one place, as well as a high level view of their treasury. I ideated with the lead product manager to come up with ways to present this information. We indexed hard on input from the few experts and customers we met with, and mocked up prototypes to put in front of real folks. I created a Transactions UI that pulled transaction data from different systems, including gas fees and costs.

All Crypto Financial Activity in One Place

The Transactions page would sync the financial activity from the user's various wallets and exchanges. Users would be able to quickly see the token, transaction hash, transaction category, from and to addresses, memos, and chart of account mappings. They'd also be able to edit some of these, which would then reflect in their company's accounting software (usually Quickbooks or Xero).

Early prototype of the Transactions page—the bread and butter of the Tactic platform.

burning question

Now that users can see this information, what do they need to do with it?

One thing we learned was that users often categorize their transactions. Each transaction must be mapped to a category, usually based on directionality, so that it gets properly accounted for in their journals. Since this is a high frequency touchpoint, I designed an interaction where users can categorize transactions individually inline or via bulk.

In addition to directional categories, there are also sub-categories. For example, a transaction's direction could be considered "money in", but buy, income, and airdrop are sub-categories that fall under that. Since the system can only detect the direction, we made it easier for users to categorize by showing only the sub-categories based on a direction. If they really wanted to, users could also change the direction in an advanced workflow.

Categorizing a transaction has never been easier!

Save Hours by Automating with Rules

For customers with huge volumes of transactions, categorizing new transactions each time could get tiring. We took the feature a step further, and came up with a way to automate the categorizing of transactions based on conditions. I designed a Rules feature where the system will perform an action (i.e. categorizing transactions or attaching a memo) if a transaction fulfills a certain criteria. Users have the choice to apply the rules retroactively as well, giving them full control of how they want to manage their transactions.

Create rules to have the system do the work for you.

outcomes
