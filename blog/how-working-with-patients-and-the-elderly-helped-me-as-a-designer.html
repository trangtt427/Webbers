How Working with Patients and the Elderly Helped Me as a Designer

December 2, 2019

Paying homage to my previous journey in healthcare.

Image Source: Thinkstock

Sympathizing at an Early Age

While growing up, my mom often took me to Oakland Chinatown to do her grocery shopping because we didn't have a lot of local Asian markets. We lived about 30 miles away from Oakland, so naturally for a young child, it felt like a mini-roadtrip each time. Oakland Chinatown also felt a whole lot different from where we livedâ€” it's a lively and noisy metropolis with many elderly Chinese and Vietnamese folks. There was also a handful of homeless people along the sidewalks, some of them asking for money from passersby and local businesses. I remember walking beside my mom and her pulling me away to stay clear of the homeless hanging around on the sidewalks. As a kid, I was very curious about them and how they ended up in their situations. Regardless, I was overcome with sadness thinking about how these people didn't have families or access to food and shelter.

I was exposed to other people's misfortunes at such a young age. The interest of wanting to help others was nurtured in me as a result. I thought to myself, "when I grow up, I want to be able to help people". I started working towards that goal.

Bonding With the Assisted Living Residents

After college, I moved back home and took on a job at an assisted living facility in Walnut Creek, the same place I worked at prior to attending UCSB. This time, I worked as a medication technician. My day to day included taking vitals of residents, managing and distributing their medications, charting on their health, and communicating with their families and healthcare providers. I wanted to work there because it served as a way to obtain patient care hours in order to apply to a Physician Assistant Program, and it gave me a chance to see my old residents again.

While the work wasn't pretty and the pay was rather low, the exposure I got from the job was eye-opening and really reinforced the truth that I care a lot about others. I found myself engaging with my residents more than what was needed of my job because many of them didn't have families who visited regularly. During my med pass, I would spend extra time with them, whether it was listening to their stories and needs or just talking about something as fleeting such as the weather. Sure, I could have used the extra time to relax in the breakroom, but there was something incredibly valuable with being able to interact with the residents. As a byproduct of that, I was able to cater to each resident's needs in terms of the way I approach them to give them their medication or extra care.

Volunteering at the Clinic

Around the time that I was working at the assisted living facility, I was also volunteering twice a month at a RotaCare clinic, a community clinic that provides free healthcare services to uninsured families and individuals with limited ability to pay for medical care. I documented patient history and took vitals, ran hemoglobin A1c tests, and prepared patients for the physician visit. Many of our patients were non-native English speakers, with more than 75% speaking primarily Spanish. Some patients had young children who spoke English and served as translators for their parents.

One of the best things that came out of this was seeing how grateful our patients were at the end of the night. Many of them were new to the states with limited resources and access to information, while some immigrated only several years ago and were struggling to make ends meet. Sometimes it was difficult to get the truth out of some of our patients. I had to earn their trust by ensuring that I was there to listen and to help. I learned a lot about how to communicate with people from different backgrounds. It really hit home for me because my parents are immigrants. I imagined that their experience was similar to what these patients are going currently going through. When working with these patients, I would ask my fellow Spanish-speaking volunteers to ask the patients questions detailing their backgrounds and symptoms. I made sure to carefully understand their stories, emotions, and struggles in order to provide the best possible care.

Empathy, Empathy, Empathy

I have since then departed from my journey in healthcare because I discovered my true passion lies in helping others in a way where I truly feel in my element: UX design. It's something I can enjoy being a part of for the rest of my life. It was right up my alley because it is an area that focuses on empathy, creativity, and doing the most for others.

I always reflect back to the years prior to me being a UX designer, when I was working closely with folks who had limited access to certain resources. I've always compared it to working with users in my role as a designer. To craft solutions that are effective and truly user-centric, I have to put myself in their shoes to learn about their needs. A lot of what I'm doing now reminds me what I used to do a few years back. Working with the elderly and patients have prepared me for this, whether it was documenting their patient stories or assessing their health. I found the skills to help me succeed as an effective communicator because of these experiences.

The Lessons I've Learned

To become a better designer, one doesn't necessarily need to read about the latest trends in UX design or attend expensive design conventions. We are designing for people, not screens. We should surround ourselves with people, especially those with different backgrounds. Once that happens, then we can truly understand the gravity of what other people's emotions and needs out of our products. We learn about what makes people tick, what delights them, and what they want to achieve in using a product. We learn to become patient and empathetic when surrounding ourselves with others. I was lucky enough to learn this through my early experiences working with my patients and residents.
