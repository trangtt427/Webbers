What I tell Others About What I Do for a Living

Product Design, plain English, and the version of my job I save for my mom.

April 20, 2020

“What do you do for work?”

I must have answered that question a hundred different ways. Sometimes I go deep and try to do the work justice, attempting to paint a very clear picture of the world I live in professionally. Sometimes I'm not in the mood to talk or realize the person I'm talking to has little knowledge of technology, and I reach for the shortest but somewhat true thing I can say. For my mom, that thing is usually: “I do computer stuff” (or the equivalent in Vietnamese). She says “oh” (or the equivalent in Vietnamese). The conversation moves on. Everyone wins.

This post is the longer answer: how I describe product design if I'm being earnest, how I got here, and why “computer stuff” isn't wrong; it's just my cop-out answer.

How I usually explain Product Design

I start with the product part. A product, in the software world, is usually an app or a website people use to get something done, like book a trip, pay a bill, message a friend, or do their job. I usually pick a popular service that the audience might already have experience with.

I tell people I help teams figure out how software should behave, what people need, where they get stuck, what order things should happen in, and what the screens should communicate so the whole thing feels clear instead of confusing. If they want a single phrase, I say something like “I design how digital products work and feel, not just how they look.” I most definitely emphasize that last part.

That's still a mouthful so I like using the “building a house” analogy. I compare it to architecture. You're not only picking paint colors; you're deciding where the doors go so people don't get lost. To drive the point across with the analogy, there's also a lot that goes into deciding about these doors. What door design is the most accessible to certain folks? How many doors? If I want to be extra colorful, I'll liken design to creating a blueprint of the house, so that engineers and other folks know exactly how to build it and with what materials. It's all about creating something with intention to help others get something done, usually in the most cost-effective way.

What the job actually looks like week to week

If someone wants more detail, I talk about what working actually looks like. I spend a lot of time in meetings with product partners about priorities, with engineers about what's feasible and what we're trading off, with researchers or customer-facing teammates about what we're learning from users, and sometimes with users themselves. I sketch flows, prototype ideas, and test them to see where they might not work.

I also care about the craft like typography, spacing, motion, and the tiny things that really elevate a product or service. The part of my work where I create beautiful interfaces is only a tiny sliver of what I actually do, because the part that's hardest to see from the outside is judgment—all the meetings and decision-making about what not to build, what to simplify, and what can be negotiated.

It's hard to convey this in a short pitch or small talk. That's why I'm grateful when people meet me halfway with curiosity and wanting to learn more. When I do get blank stares though, I don't take it personally. Job titles in tech are a mess.

My path here

I didn't grow up wanting to become a product designer. I didn't even know what it was until my mid 20s. I did, however, have an interest in visual arts and an appreciation for immersive experiences due to my growing up playing lots of video games and watching cartoons. I was confident in my ability to discern things that look and felt good, from things that don't. So, my journey was a winding line through visual design via Photoshop in high school, trying my hand at a medical-adjacent job because $$$, pivoting to UX at an HVAC company, collaboration with engineers, learning to speak the language of business outcomes, and eventually landing at proper tech companies. I really enjoy letting my curiosity and urge to make things better drive, which are the things that Design really leans into. It's always “why is this difficult?” And then “I bet there's a better way to do this”.

Along the way, I learned that Design isn't a checklist nor is it linear. It's a way of making decisions—often messy, often negotiated—with other people who might care about different risks but share the same goals. The longer I've done this, the more I respect that social layer. The pixels are just the visible part of my job, but the behind the scenes alignment of people is the hardest to show.

When I keep it simple

For family gatherings, small talk, or anyone who doesn't need the full map: I still say I work with computers. I help build the products and services that people use. I've learned that I don't need to talk about Figma at every chance. I don't think the shorter version is a complete disservice. The full version of what I do lives here, and in the conversations where the stakes are higher. Both are true.
