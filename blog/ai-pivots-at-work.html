Keeping Track of the Pivots

Reflections on being nudged to use AI at work, exploring in Claude, and the difficulty of tracking where things went.

Date
March 2025
Status
Draft

We were being nudged to use AI.

At my current company, there's a growing push to incorporate AI into our workflows. It's not mandatory—yet—but the nudges are everywhere. Slack channels dedicated to AI tips. Internal demos showing how to speed up tasks. Leadership talking about "embracing the future." The message is clear: we should be exploring this.

I started exploring in Claude.

So I did. I've been using Claude for brainstorming, for drafting, for thinking through design problems. It's useful. Sometimes it feels like having a sharp junior designer in the room—one who doesn't get tired and can hold a lot of context. Other times it feels like talking to someone who's read every design book but hasn't shipped a product.

What I didn't expect was how quickly the conversations would branch. A single thread becomes three. One of those becomes five. I'll pivot from "help me structure this user flow" to "actually, let me reconsider the problem" to "wait, what if we framed it differently?" And then I can't remember which branch had the good idea.

The pivots are hard to keep track of.

That's the thing I'm finding difficult: keeping track of all the pivots. The AI is great at following me when I change direction. It doesn't complain. It doesn't say "we were just talking about something else." It just… pivots. And I keep pivoting. And somewhere in the middle of all that, there might have been a better direction that we never fully explored.

I don't have a solution yet. Maybe it's better note-taking. Maybe it's being more deliberate about when I allow myself to pivot. Maybe it's accepting that some threads will be dead ends and that's okay. Or maybe the tools will get better at surfacing "you had an interesting idea three branches ago."

For now, I'm just noticing it. And writing it down.
