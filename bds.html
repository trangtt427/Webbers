A rich and organized design system that's customizable and easy to use. Live on Figma Community.

Summary

Built a design system that includes dozens of components and 600+ variants

Currently maintained with new components being added monthly

opportunity

A Baby is Born

A company I worked for built a design system as part of a mega redesign, but as soon as multiple designers started working out of it, we learned that it wasn't as efficient when designing at scale. We ended up rebuilding some of the components as Figma variants to smooth out the workflows for designers and engineers. Throughout the process, I became increasingly curious and inspired.

"What's it like to build a design system from scratch?"

I wanted to try my hand at creating my own design system; one that is extensive, simple to use, and optimized for repeatability. For a month, I dedicated a dozen hours a week towards this personal project—which I lovingly dubbed Baby Design System.

solutions

One Component, Many Styles

My company's design system had too many clunky components for different states and styles of the same asset. We had two different button components for size; one small and one big. We even had components made with extra layers just so that they could be hacked for certain permutations. I aimed to be more mindful of this for Baby Design System, so I created each component to have as many customizable properties as possible. This elegantly cut down on the number of components, staying true to a clean and intentional design system.

Style just about anything for a button using a master component.

I applied the same principle to more complex components like tables. Properties could be changed at the table level, and at the nested row level. Each row item could be swapped between text, actions, statuses, and avatars.

Tables can be customized down to each row item.

Clean Documentation

Navigating through the company's design system was a challenge. Sometimes I'd uncover a component I was supposed to use, way after implementing what I thought was the right one. Other times I'd be using the right component, but would have to fiddle with the layers to get the style I wanted. For Baby Design System, I made it easier by documenting all possible levels of customization offered. Colors, typography, icons, components and variants—everything is organized and documented clearly so that people don't need to second guess.

learnings

A Growing Baby

Nothing I've done here is groundbreaking since this is typically how a design system comes to life in Figma. Is it a comprehensive system? Depends on what you need. There are lots of free design systems out there that are loaded with hundreds of components and styles. But if you want to design quickly and need just the basics—buttons, forms, alerts, tabs, even tables—then this design system is for you!

As satiating as this was for me, I think designers should resist the temptation to reinvent the wheel with new design systems at work. It may not be cost-efficient for a new company to go all in on their own design system as one of their first pursuits. Using one of the many popular systems out there could be a viable option depending on the business needs. What's more important than knowing how to create a design system is knowing how to use one.
