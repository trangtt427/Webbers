opportunity

A Baby is Born

A design system is a collection of a company's reusable components and guidelines that tells designers what, how, and when to use certain patterns. Instead of reinventing the wheel each time a new feature comes along, designers can use what's already been validated and established, ensuring quality and consistency across the different products offered by the company. This also saves internal teams a whole lot of time when building. At one company, we had a design system in place that was used to develop one of our products, but once multiple designers started leveraging it for their respective projects, we soon learned that the system was limited when designing at scale.

I spent a lot of time hunting for components in this system and trying to make sense of what I was allowed to use. I was curious about the thought process that led to the components being constructed and organized in the way that they were and thought about what I would have done differently. This inspired me. I wanted to try my hand at creating a personal design system from scratch; one that is equipped with the essentials, clearly documented, and easy to customize. For a month, I dedicated a dozen hours a week towards this new design system—which I lovingly named "Baby Design System".

solutions

Flexible Components For All Your Needs

The design system at my company was overloaded with redundancy; similar but separate components existed for their different styles. If I had a small button component in front of me but wanted to use a larger size, I'd naturally look to see if there were any variants. If not, I would search and see if that option was even available. I sought to avoid this in Baby Design System. I wanted to offer more visibility into what permutations exist through a single master component and make it easy to swap between them. Don't like rectangle buttons? Toggle to round buttons. Want to change the primary color of your button? Change it upstream to the color of your choice and watch it proliferate for all buttons. With just a single component, users have the controls for all of its attributes in one place.

All you need is a single component for all your buttons

For more complex components like tables, I exposed the ability to change the nested component properties at the parent level. In addition to changing the table, you'll be able to easily customize what the row items are and swap between the variants.

Fine-tune complex components like tables

Documentation to Help You Find Your Way

My experience with my company's design system compelled me to take design documentation seriously. It wasn't immediately clear to me if I was looking at a master component or one of its instances. I was bouncing around the Figma file trying to locate guidelines and what options were available to me. With Baby Design System, I've organized everything in a way where users can instantly see what the different customizations are. Also included are anchors to help users jump to the master components. My documentation took a page from Ant Design, a popular enterprise-grade design system that I've enjoyed and used extensively in the past. Another helpful tool for documentation was a plugin called Foundation: Color Generator, which I used to assemble some neat color palettes that show the contrast ratios for each color.

Foundation: Color Generator has a feature that nicely plots your color profiles and their contrast ratios

Variant properties are documented for each component and users can navigate to master components

learnings

A Growing Baby

Nothing I've done here is groundbreaking; it's the typical process one would go through to create a design system. Getting hands on with building Baby Design System was still a rewarding experience though. Is it a comprehensive system? Not at all! There are plenty of free design systems out there that are far more mature with thousands of variants. But if you're someone who wants to focus on prototyping instead of building custom components and needs just the basics—buttons, forms, alerts, tabs, even tables—this design system will have you covered.

Creating (and even using) a design system properly requires a lot of discipline and planning, just like building a real product for people to use. Through this endeavor, I've learned what it takes to craft components down to their pixel-perfect details, when not to create variants, and to consider accessibility frequently. And for the first time, I got to design for an interesting type of user; the designer who swears by Figma. What started off as a personal experiment is now a living, breathing, and growing passion project. I'm still adding more to Baby Design System, including handmade icons and illustrations at some point. Coming soon to Figma Community!
