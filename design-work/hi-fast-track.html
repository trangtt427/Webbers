Human Interest

Designing the industry's first 401(k) self-serve onboarding experience.

Role

Design Lead

highlights

Proved that the market was ready for self-serve

Business Problem

Admins signing up for our 401(k)s were required to meet with Onboarding Specialists to finalize their plan—even for the simplest and most straightforward plans. This antiquated process was expensive for us and frustrating for admins. This prompted one of our top partner payroll providers to demand self-serve onboarding in order to be in good standing with them.

Process Overview

  • Formed strategy to focus on single employer startup plans
  • Led ideation exercise with CXO and Product to jam on concepts
  • Refined concepts into detailed designs and established Design Principles
  • Spun up a research plan for usability testing
  • Evolve current Design System to support new surfaces
  • QA and tested against current systems

Concepting

Designing a 401(k) plan can get very nuanced and it's possible that an admin may want to customize every part of it (eligibility, employer match, vesting schedules, etc.) but we learned from our data that most trend towards an industry-standard configuration.

Instead of overwhelming users with questions about designing every part of their plan from scratch, we'd offer curated plan designs. Onboarding would just be a "click next" experience for admins, all the way through bank connection and signing plan docs.

Lofi concepts of the onboarding flow.

Usability Testing

Up until now, designs were informed mainly by our own bubble of assumptions. My Product partner and I teamed up with our UXR expert to get our designs in front of admins via moderated user studies.

Our goal was to see if the admins could get all the way to (fake) signing their plan docs, while evaluating how effective our solutions were.

Synthesized insights for each of the flows.

The results: Safe Harbor is mysterious. Admins couldn't see their options. The pricing was confusing.

We fell short of effectively communicating what Safe Harbor was in our designs (some thought it was a company, others thought it was a Human Interest-coined term). Language in our flow suggested that there were different tiers of service, but users had trouble finding the route to non-premium options. I presented alternative designs to stop headlining with "Safe Harbor", ease the upsell of premium options, and apply familiar ecommerce patterns.

The new Service Package page performed better in testing.
