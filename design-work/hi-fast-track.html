Human Interest

Fast Track 401(k)

Closing America's retirement gap with a streamlined experience for admins setting up their first 401(k) plan.

Role

Design Lead for Admin Experience

industry

Startups, Fintech, Benefits, B2B2C

platform

Web app

The Business Problem

Admins signing up for our 401(k)s were required to meet with Onboarding Specialists to finalize their plan—even for the simplest and most straightforward plans. This process barely changed since Human Interest's beginnings, was expensive for us, and frustrated admins coming from our partner payroll provider, whose product experience is considered the gold-standard in the industry.

Most small business admins want to set up a 401(k) without talking to a person.

The pressure was on: one our biggest partner payroll providers required us to cater to admins who want self-serve. To continue being in good standing as a partner, we needed to make this happen by the end of June 2023—less than five months away.

I led the design efforts for this initiative. Some of my achievements are listed below:

Led Design workshops. I facilitated a design-thinking session with our Customer Experience Officer and Director of Product Marketing to start breaking down the business opportunity.

Conducted research and usability testing. We fortified our designs with feedback from users and implementation specialists.

Launched Fast Track 401(k). The product was the first of its kind in the market, indicating that users don't want to be on a call with a human for every little thing.

Forming the Strategy

There weren't enough resources or time to make self-serve happen for all customers, so it was important to double down on a subset of plans in order to make this achievable by the deadline.

We wanted to target single employers who are offering their first 401(k) plan.

We share the same personas as our partner payroll provider, so we would rely on them to pre-qualify admins for this new experience. When signing up for a 401(k), admins are considering costs and making huge decisions around things like:

  • Eligibility
  • Employer contributions
  • Minimum service required
  • Vesting schedules
  • Auto-enrollment (and default contribution rates)

Designing a 401(k) plan can get very nuanced and it's possible that an admin may want to customize every part of it, but we learned from our data that most trend towards an industry-standard configuration. The project lead, the CXO, assembled a core team of Product Management, Product Marketing, Engineering, and myself to drive this transformation. Our goal was to have a high start-to-complete rate while delivering something that we'd be proud of.

Group Brainstorm

I kicked off a 2.5 hour-long whiteboarding session with the CXO and Director of Product Marketing. During this exploration, we bounced ideas, shared knowledge from our respective domains, and riffed off of each another.

We landed on a concept that would shape our vision of a no-human-required product (and the way we handled big projects).

Instead of overwhelming users with questions about designing every part of their plan from scratch, we'd offer curated plan designs. Onboarding would just be a "click next" experience for admins, all the way through bank connection and signing plan docs.

Concepting

I transformed our whiteboarding concepts into wireframes and gathered feedback from Onboarding Specialists, the Integrations Team, Legal, and other key stakeholders. Our proposals checked off the boxes of removing industry jargon and offering plenty of in-app education.

A few of the onboarding screens in Figma.

To make sure we stay consistent and true to our vision, we aligned on a set of Design Principles.

  • Simplicity. More options to choose from lead to anxiety and indecision. We wanted every pixel to have justification.
  • Guidance. Everything would be defaulted, steering users towards maximizing 401(k) value.
  • Inspire confidence. Framing decisions in a familiar way and providing social proof would help admins trust the product.

The new designs aimed to radically simplify the setup flow.

We only ask Admins a few choices about their plan design, thanks to integrations with our partner to pull in the admin's profile and defaulted selections backed by data.

Putting Our Ideas in Front of Users

Up until now, designs were informed mainly by our own bubble of assumptions. My Product partner and I teamed up with our UXR expert to get our designs in front of admins via moderated user studies. With a structured research prompt in hand, clickable prototypes polished, and a framework for synthesizing insights, we were ready to see how our solutions would fare with six admin recruits.

Our goal was to see if the admins could get all the way to (fake) signing their plan docs, while evaluating how effective our solutions were.

Users thought that the flow was intuitive and easy to follow, but there were a few threats to our start-to-complete rate:

Safe Harbor is mysterious. Admins couldn't see their options. The pricing was confusing.

We fell short of effectively communicating what Safe Harbor was in our designs (some thought it was a company, others thought it was a Human Interest-coined term). Language in our flow suggested that there were different tiers of service, but users had trouble finding the route to non-premium options.

I presented alternative designs to stop headlining with "Safe Harbor", ease the upsell of premium options, and apply familiar ecommerce patterns.

The new Service Package page performed better in testing.

Detailed designs with visual design applied.

End to End Testing

With all of our systems linked up (Salesforce, Chili Piper, and our partner payroll provider demo environment), our cross-functional team came together to test the whole process. I aided in these sessions by organizing a list of admin scenarios to run through.

In our tests, we had our cross-functional team members validate the following events in realtime:

  • User information captured in Salesforce.
  • Plan match preferences.
  • Service package selection.
  • If the user is/isn't an authorized signer, and if plan docs were sent/signed.
  • Plaid integration.

The Salesforce journey begins at the account creation step.

Outcomes

After going live with a beta phase, the product was made available to everyone in September 2023. Read more about the release announcement here.

Human Interest successfully made it as a preferred partner and we started seeing an influx of signups right after launch.
