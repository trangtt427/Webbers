Human Interest

Making 401(k)s accessible to people in all lines of work, starting by empowering admins to easily set one up through a wholly self-serve experience.

Summary

Deepened partner relations with a fully integrated experience

Launched the company's first end-to-end self-serve workflow for admins

Successful start-to-complete rate for companies that wanted simple 401(k) plans

Opportunity

Most small businesses wanted to set up a 401(k) without needing to speak to a human.

Admins signing up for 401(k)s weren't happy being bounced between multiple salesfolk, a process that remained unchanged since Human Interest's inception. Every customer—even for the most simple and straightforward plans—was forced to speak to a specialist in order to finish onboarding. This was costly for the company and frustrating for admins coming from our partner payroll provider, whose product experience is considered the gold-standard in the industry.

The current onboarding experience was full of industry jargon and lacked guidance for admins.

The pressure was on—our partner payroll provider required us to offer a seamless self-serve experience that upholds their high standards. To continue being a "preferred partner", we needed to make this happen by the end of June 2023.

define

We didn't have enough time or resources to make self-serve happen for all customers, so we focused on the most common use cases for now: simple, first-time 401(k) plans.

Our strategy was to double down on a subset of plans in order to make self-serve achievable by the deadline. 401(k)s are wildly complex; there are thousands of possible combinations that involve:

  • Eligibility
  • Employer contributions
  • Minimum service required
  • Vesting
  • Auto-enrollment (and default contributions)

It's possible for admins to want to customize everything in their 401(k) plan, but we learned from our data that most opt for a common plan type. If there was a place where one option was more popular based on our data, we would default it.

design

I led a 2-hour whiteboarding session with core members of the project team, and at the end of it, landed on a vision that would shape the future of self-serve (and the way we worked on big projects).

During this exploration, we bounced ideas off of each other, building upon each other's proposals, and ideated around guidelines and patterns that would keep us consistent with how we make decisions for this project.

Using Excalidraw, core members were able to throw their ideas on a canvas without worrying about formatting and polish.

From this session, we came up with the following Design Principles:

Simplicity

More options to choose from lead to anxiety and indecision. We wanted every pixel to have justification.

Guidance

Everything would be defaulted, steering users towards maximizing 401(k) value.

Inspire Confidence

Framing decisions in a familiar way and providing social proof would help admins land on the best outcomes.

I converted our beloved rough sketches into wireframes, and opened up the floodgates for feedback from our important project stakeholders. The designs went through refinements week after week.

While still at low fidelity, the wireframes took enough of an elegant form to be able to solicit feedback from people.

Just a few steps needed to set up a 401(k).

The flow was radically simplified. Admins are asked to make only a few explicit choices, with the recommended ones being defaulted—a solution made confident thanks to data and insights from our onboarding specialists.

There's a lot for anyone to wrap their heads around when it comes to 401(k)s. We've provided plenty of in-app education and pathways to heavier educational resources through tooltips.

user research

My Product partner and I teamed up with our UXR expert to get our designs in front of admin eyes, through moderated user studies.

With a structured research prompt in hand, clickable prototypes ready, and a framework for synthesizing insights, we watched how five recruited admins fared with our solutions. We were interested in seeing if the admins could complete the flow, and what their Single Ease Question (SEQ) ratings would be. While the SEQ scores were very favorable at the end of each call, it became increasingly obvious which things would threaten our start-to-complete rate.

Risky Areas

Safe Harbor

In our calls, no one knew exactly what this meant. Some thought it was a company, some thought it was a Human Interest-coined term. But most would Google it.

Confusing Service Options

Users were wary about the upsell that was happening. Language in our flow suggested that there were different tiers of service, but users had trouble finding the non-premium options.

Inconsistent Pricing

Our anchoring on the price of our cheapest service while upselling the premium service was not well-received by users. They wanted to know what they were getting exactly.

Designs were improved after each session. As a result, we stopped headlining with "Safe Harbor", eased the aggressive upsell of premium options, and applied familiar ecommerce patterns that users expected in their purchasing journey.
