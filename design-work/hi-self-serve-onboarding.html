Human Interest

Making 401(k)s accessible to people in all lines of work, starting with empowering admins to easily set one up through a self-serve experience.

Role

  • Visual design, interaction design, UI design
  • UX research
  • Evolved current design system
  • Internal branding and creative direction

Results

Launched the industry's first self-serve 401(k) onboarding

Deepened partner relations with a fully embedded experience

Successful start-to-complete rate for companies that wanted simple 401(k) plans

Application

Web and mobile web

Opportunity

Most small businesses wanted to set up a 401(k) without needing to speak to a human.

Admins signing up for our 401(k)s weren't happy being bounced between multiple salesfolk and onboarding specialists, a process that remained unchanged since the company's beginning. Every customer was forced to speak with a specialist in order to finish onboarding—even for the simplest and most straightforward plans. This was expensive for us and frustrating for admins coming from our partner payroll provider, whose product experience is considered the gold-standard in the industry.

The current onboarding experience was full of industry jargon and lacked guidance for admins.

The pressure was on—our partner payroll provider required us to offer a seamless self-serve experience on par with their standards. To continue being a "preferred partner", we needed to make this happen by the end of June 2023, less than five months away.

define

There wasn't enough time or resources to make self-serve happen for all customers, so we focused on the most common use cases for now: simple, first-time 401(k) plans.

Our strategy was to double down on a subset of plans in order to make self-serve achievable by the deadline. We serve the same personas as the payroll provider, so we we would rely on them to "pre-qualify" admins for this new experience. 401(k)s are wildly complex; there are thousands of possible combinations that involve:

  • Eligibility
  • Employer contributions
  • Minimum service required
  • Vesting
  • Auto-enrollment (and default contribution rates)

It's possible that an admin may want to customize everything in their 401(k) plan, but we learned from our data that most lean towards an industry-standard plan type.

Our project lead, the CXO, put together a core team of Product Management, Product Marketing, Engineering, and myself to drive this transformative initiative. We enforced regular team syncs, sprint reports, and stakeholder decks to keep everyone informed and aligned.

design

I kicked off a 2.5 hour-long whiteboarding session with core members of the project team, and at the end of it, landed on a vision that would shape the future of self-serve (and the way we handled big projects).

During this design-facilitated exploration, we bounced ideas off of each other, innovated, and made big decisions. Instead of overwhelming users with questions about plan customization, we'd offer curated plan designs of popular settings.

Using Excalidraw, core members were able to throw their ideas on a canvas without worrying about formatting and polish.

We even came up with the following Design Principles:

Simplicity

More options to choose from lead to anxiety and indecision. We wanted every pixel to have justification.

Guidance

Everything would be defaulted, steering users towards maximizing 401(k) value.

Inspire Confidence

Framing decisions in a familiar way and providing social proof would help admins feel good about what they're doing.

I translated our rough concepts into clean wireframes, and opened up the floodgates for feedback from our important project stakeholders. The designs went through refinements week after week.

While still in low fidelity, the wireframes took enough of an elegant form to be able to get the point across to stakeholders.

Just a few steps were needed to set up a 401(k) with us.

The flow was radically simple. Admins are asked to make only a few choices about their plan design, thanks to our payroll integration technology, and defaulted options—a solution made solid thanks to data and insights from our onboarding specialists.

Additional Information at Your Disposal

There's a lot for anyone to wrap their heads around when it comes to 401(k)s, like compliance testing and tax credits. We've provided plenty of in-app education and pathways to educational resources. Again, we would leverage social proof to provide the value of certain decisions, so admins wouldn't need to resort to these heavier pieces of content.

user research

Up until now, designs were informed mainly by our own bubble of assumptions. My Product partner and I teamed up with our UXR expert to get our designs in front of admins via moderated user studies.

With a structured research prompt in hand, clickable prototypes polished, and a framework for synthesizing insights, we were ready to see how our solutions would fare with six admin recruits. We were interested in seeing if the admins could get all the way to fake-sign their docs, and what their Single Ease Question (SEQ) ratings would be. While the SEQ scores were very favorable at the end of each call, it became increasingly obvious which items would threaten our start-to-complete rate.

User insights

Risky Areas

Safe Harbor is mysterious.

Safe Harbor is a type of 401(k) plan that allows companies to avoid regulations and expenses, but it wasn't clear in our flow. Some thought it was a company, some thought it was a Human Interest-coined term. But everyone would Google it, opening up chances for drop-off.

Admins wanted to see their options.

Language in our flow suggested that there were different tiers of service, but users had trouble finding the route to non-premium options.

The pricing was confusing.

Our anchoring on the price of our cheapest service while upselling the premium service was not well-received by users. They wanted to know what they were getting for their money.

Designs were improved after each session. We stopped headlining with industry jargon like "Safe Harbor", eased the aggressive upsell of premium options, and applied familiar ecommerce patterns that are typical in modern purchasing journeys.

challenges

The project had great momentum to start, but it wasn't always smooth sailing.

While the admin self-serve initiative had the support of the board and Leadership, we weren't shielded from the everyday disruptions and chaos of being a startup. We lost engineering resources to contending fires, and required a lot of focused coordination from teams like Data, Revenue Ops, and Growth to come up with a plan around handling Salesforce pipeline stages. This project was at the intersection of many things, but fortunately, was a big enough opportunity to get reprioritized as the company's highest initiative.

At one point, the designs evolved so frequently that the Dev team couldn't figure out what changes needed to happen. I came up with the Design Log to capture all the wireframe changes and product decisions, and keep parity with the Product Requirements Doc.
