Human Interest

Making 401(k)s accessible to people in all lines of work, starting with empowering admins to set one up through a self-serve experience.

Role

  • Visual design, interaction design, UI design
  • UX research
  • Evolved the current design system
  • Internal branding and creative direction
  • Product Manager's sidekick

Results

Launched the industry's first self-serve 401(k) onboarding

Deepened partner relations with a fully embedded experience

Successful start-to-complete rate for companies that wanted simple 401(k) plans

Application

Web and mobile web

Opportunity

Most small business admins want to set up a 401(k) without talking to a person.

Admins signing up for our 401(k)s weren't happy being bounced between multiple salesfolk and Onboarding Specialists, a process that barely changed since the company's beginning. Every customer was forced to join a call with someone from Human Interest to finalize their plan setup—even for the simplest and most straightforward plans. This was expensive for us and frustrating for admins coming from our partner payroll provider, whose product experience is considered the gold-standard in the industry.

The current onboarding experience was full of industry jargon and lacked guidance for admins.

The pressure was on: our partner payroll provider required us to offer a seamless self-serve sign-up experience on par with their standards. To continue being a "preferred partner", we needed to make this happen by the end of June 2023—less than five months away.

define

There weren't enough resources or time to make self-serve happen for all customers, so we focused on the most common use cases for now: single employers who are offering their first 401(k) plan.

Our strategy was to double down on a subset of plans in order to make self-this achievable by the deadline. We share the same personas as the payroll provider, so we would rely on them to "pre-qualify" admins for this new experience. When signing up for a 401(k), admins are considering costs and making huge decisions around things like:

  • Eligibility
  • Employer contributions
  • Minimum service required
  • Vesting schedules
  • Auto-enrollment (and default contribution rates)

Designing a 401(k) plan can get very nuanced and it's possible that an admin may want to customize every part of it, but we learned from our data that most tend to lean towards an industry-standard configuration.

The project lead, the CXO, assembled a core team of Product Management, Product Marketing, Engineering, and myself to drive this transformation. Our goal was to have a high start-to-complete rate while delivering something that we'd be proud of.

design

I kicked off a 2.5 hour-long whiteboarding session with the CXO and Product, and at the end of it, landed on a concept that would shape our vision of a no-human-interaction product (and the way we handled big projects).

During this design-facilitated exploration, we bounced ideas off of each other, shared knowledge from each of our respective domains, and innovated. Instead of overwhelming users with questions about designing every part of their plan from scratch, we'd offer curated plan designs of popular settings. Onboarding would just be a "click next" experience for admins, all the way through bank connection and signing plan docs.

We threw down ideas in Excalidraw and aligned on a flow that lightened the cognitive load for admins while selling some of our best plan options.

To make sure we stayed consistent and true to our vision, we came up with the following Design Principles:

Simplicity

More options to choose from lead to anxiety and indecision. We wanted every pixel to have justification.

Guidance

Everything would be defaulted, steering users towards maximizing 401(k) value.

Inspire Confidence

Framing decisions in a familiar way and providing social proof would help admins trust the product.

I translated our rough concepts into clean wireframes and gathered feedback from Onboarding Specialists, the Integrations Team, Legal, and other project stakeholders. Designs were refined week after week.

There's a lot for anyone to wrap their heads around when it comes to 401(k)s, like compliance testing and tax credits. We've provided plenty of in-app education and pathways to educational resources. Again, we would leverage social proof to show the value of certain decisions so that admins won't need to resort to heavier content.

user research

Up until now, designs were informed mainly by our own bubble of assumptions. My Product partner and I teamed up with our UXR expert to get our designs in front of admins via moderated user studies.

With a structured research prompt in hand, clickable prototypes polished, and a framework for synthesizing insights, we were ready to see how our solutions would fare with six admin recruits. We were interested in seeing if the admins could get all the way to fake-sign their plan docs, and what their Single Ease Question (SEQ) ratings would be.

We captured reactions, emotions, testimonies, and interesting insights on each screen.

Users thought that the flow was intuitive and easy to follow, but we realized there were a few threats to our start-to-complete rate:

Safe Harbor is mysterious.

Safe Harbor is a type of 401(k) plan that allows companies to avoid regulations and expenses, but we missed the mark in our design. Some thought it was a company, some thought it was a Human Interest-coined term. People would Google it, making this a risk for drop-off.

Admins couldn't see their options.

Language in our flow suggested that there were different tiers of service, but users had trouble finding the route to non-premium options.

The pricing was confusing.

Our anchoring on the price of our cheapest service while upselling the premium service did not resonate with users. They wanted to know what they were getting for their money.

I created alternative designs after each session. We stopped headlining with industry jargon like "Safe Harbor", eased the aggressive upsell of premium options, and applied familiar ecommerce patterns.

We had the recruits go through the original prototype before having them assess the alternatives. The later versions were preferred.

challenges

The project had great momentum to start, but it wasn't always smooth sailing.

While this initiative had the support of the board and Leadership, we weren't shielded from the everyday disruptions and chaos of being a startup. We lost engineering resources to contending fires, and required more coordinated efforts than we thought we needed from Data, Revenue Ops, and Growth because of technology limitations. This project was at the intersection of many things, but fortunately, was a big enough opportunity to get reprioritized as the company's highest initiative.

At one point, the designs evolved so frequently that the Dev team couldn't figure out what changes needed to happen. I proposed a Design Log to capture all the wireframe changes and product decisions, and keep parity with the Product Requirements Doc.

Outcomes
