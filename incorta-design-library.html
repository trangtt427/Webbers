Incorta: Design Library

The single source of truth for design at Incorta

Project Background

About Incorta

Incorta is a hyper-converged data analytics app that removes the ETL process in data warehousing and lets users make use of their data fast. Our app allows users to bring in data to create connections between tables, and ultimately, create data visualizations so that businesses can make key decisions.

The Problem

In my first few days of starting Incorta, I immediately learned that we didn't have easy access to our design components. Icons, fonts, and colors were scattered between dozens of Sketch files and Zeplin screens. As a result of this, it made it difficult to be consistent with our designs, especially between the Egypt design team and the front-end engineers. My lead knew about such pain points before I came onboard. Since I had experience with design systems, my lead tasked me with creating a unified Sketch library to solve these issues.

Besides optimizing the workflow amongst designers and engineers, the design lead argued that having an Incorta design language would help us look like a more "mature" company. Also by focusing on one of our design core values of consistency, having a certain style of components appear globally on our app would help users understand the Incorta language. Users would be able to recognize such patterns on different parts of the app through repetition, and remember the interactions associated with them.

Role

Because we didn't have a dedicated visual designer on the team, I was the owner of the library and synced up with the design lead regularly to review and commit on modifications made to our assets. I reached out to some of our front-end engineers and designers to locate assets.

The Frameworks We Used

Migrating From Angular to React

As an early start-up, we didn't have the bandwidth to create a design system from scratch. While we were designing new features for the core product, the front-end engineers were also porting the entire app into the React framework in parallel.

The old Data page in Angular

The Data page after it was ported into React

Using a Modified Ant Library

We were using the Ant Library Sketch plug-in to pull icons directly into our Sketch files. However, we weren't really documenting what icons were being used. It was a pain point addressed by engineers to have to go find the components and styles on the Ant Library website. It was also especially difficult for them because we were altering some of these components to fit within our app without their knowing. Overall, it was just poor design etiquette.

Engineers visited the Ant Library frequently and tried their best to match the styles we used in our design files

The Process

Auditing the Core App

The first thing I did was go through the desktop app to find the inconsistencies in our app. This meant documenting things like the different icons being used for similar actions, inconsistent colors, misalignment of pixels, incorrect border widths, wrong fonts, etc. I shared my findings with my lead and wrote up the requirements in phases, focusing on the granular bits first (color, icon, type) before pursuing the components (buttons and patterns). The deliverable would be a master Sketch library that would be shared amongst designers.

Colors

I went through the Zeplin styleguide as well as the Incorta app to collect all the colors we were using. We had colors that were fairly similar to one another because there was no color palette handed off to Engineering along with wireframes. Some high fidelity mock-ups used different hex codes unintentionally which ended up going through development without considering the existing color palette.

After collecting the colors and testing their contrast ratios on web, I met with the design lead to go over which colors we should omit. We investigated where certain colors were being used and worked up some guidelines on how to use them. I created a new Sketch file to house the final palette.

The current Incorta color palette after consolidating the app and Zeplin styles

Icons

While we had no written rules on what type of icons we should be using, there was some consistency in the type of icons that appeared in our app as we leaned towards simple icons with specific border widths. The Ant Design library, however, had multiple styles for the same icons, and we were using several of them. I came up with some proposals on which icons to replace and we synced up with figure out the different states of icons (default, hover, focused, etc.).

In a Sketch file, I had grouped icons with similar actions next to each other. It was a simple process of going through the icons and simply picking out which ones we didn't want to use, as they didn't sit well with our current suite of icons.

In some instances, we didn't have Ant Library icons of some concepts. As a result, I had to find existing icons and redesign them in Illustrator for our use. I also altered several icons to make them appear as if they came from a family of icons.

Some of the icons we borrowed from the Ant Design Library that we modified

Typography

Next, all the fonts were consolidated from the app and Zeplin projects. As we were creating new features, some new styles were needed to support heavy displays of data. I confirmed with the lead what font sizes were being used where, and added it to the Sketch library. I had written out guidelines where to use certain styles.

We consolidated similar font styles to clean do some clean-up

Components

The last phase of the design library were the components. I collected the UI patterns on our app which were modified Ant Design components and created Sketch symbols using our updated colors, fonts, and icons. They were categorized by their usage, whether they were navigation-specific symbols, table components, or used globally.

The Sketch file was made into a Sketch Library and shared amongst the other designers to use for building prototypes quickly

Uses For the Incorta Design Library

Front-end Engineers

The Incorta Sketch Library (which I nicknamed Dumplings) was shared with team members via Zeplin. The engineers slowly began cleaning up their CSS styles to make sure the color names had the same class. We received feedback from the front-end team that it was nice to have all of our assets in one place, and if the engineers had any questions on what icons to use, they'd have a place to refer back to. The icons were all made to be exportable in the Zeplin screens as well.

Design Team

While the design system helped out the engineers, it would be put to use mainly by the designers. At the time, the design team was getting ready to scale and we didn't have a mature process of collaborating designer to designer. The Sketch library I created was a good starting point, because my lead and I were heavily using it to create both lo-fi and high-fi mock-ups quickly.

A screenshot of some of the symbols

I carefully crafted the symbols so that designers using the system can easily override certain assets. The components are entirely flexible and the master file was being maintained and updated every day and shared through our Google Drive.

The Outcome

Where the Incorta Design Library Stands Today

After the design system was completed enough to be used, priorities changed and I was delegated other features to work on. However, the design system helped us in having a single source of truth when it came to controlling consistency within the Incorta product. It was especially important when multiple designers were working on the core app and had to use global patterns. Having the design system readily available as a Sketch library helped us work efficiently as we were able to pull the assets directly into our prototypes. Even though I was working on other projects, I was still updating the library whenever we ended up creating new patterns.

The Takeaway

The project took about a month. I spent a lot of time researching dozens of design systems and reading into best practices on how to deliver one. The oppportunity to come up with a design system for Incorta was an important milestone for the design team, as we were now ramping up our processes and scaling the team.

Unfortunately, I didn't get to revisit the design system as much as I would like because upper management was concerned about where we were allocating our efforts. As designers, we advocated for having a mature design system, but we had to be scrappy at a start-up, meaning they wanted us to focus on customer-facing features. To me, a company that allocates resources into maintaining a library tells me that the company cares about design. Things that I would have liked to do would include expanding the design system further to consolidate the Dark Mode UI that I created earlier as well as work with a technical writer to incorporate a unified voice in our messaging.
