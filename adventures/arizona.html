Sedona, the Grand Canyon, and Page

Arizona | October 2023

Experiencing the Southwest and its desert gems.

Days 1 & 2: Sedona

We started our first whole day in Arizona driving from Phoenix to Sedona and hiking the Bell Rock Trail. Despite being less than a mile out and back, the unassuming hike quickly pushed me to my physical limits. We found ourselves rock scrambling and relying on explosive leg movements to thrust ourselves up.

Bell Rock in the distance.

My short legs had trouble getting me up here.

It was a fun experience—I've never done anything quite like it. We took it easy for the remainder of that day, taking a route that was more of a nature walk to get back to car. On the second day, we opted for a longer hike, the West Fork of Oak Creek Trail, that took us through more diverse scenery.

Days 3 & 4: The Grand Canyon

We kicked off our official first day in the Grand Canyon by hiking a hidden gem of a trail called the Shoshone Point Trail. The parking lot is unmarked, making it extra elusive to most tourists. It was a brief hike, but at the end was a secluded viewpoint where the canyons appear to extend endlessly. For a while, we enjoyed the spot to ourselves.

The view from the Shoshone Point Trail.

That afternoon, we took a stroll on the Rim Trail. It's an accessible trail; paved and populated by crowds from all over.

Moments before sunset on the Rim Trail.

Lodging at the Grand Canyon that night meant a more intense hike the next day. We went on the South Kaibab trail toward an appropriately named vista called the Ooh Aah Point. It's an out and back trail, so the farther and lower we went, the tougher the ascent would be coming back. Along the way, we encountered seemingly harmless switchbacks...until it was time to go back up.

"Ooh Ahh" indeed.

Day 5: Horseshoe Bend, Antelope Canyon and back to Flagstaff

After driving through Navajo lands, taking a break at a trading post for Navajo tacos, and technical car troubles, we arrived at the small town of Page. We only spent one night there so that we could start our next day off with a sunrise hike to the picturesque Horseshoe Bend.

We woke up early to get a glimpse of the Horseshoe Bend around sunrise (6:30am).

With the time we had left in Page, we embarked on a group tour inside the Antelope Canyon. Since the land belongs to the Navajo Nation, tours are only possible if guided by someone from the Navajo Tribe. There were multiple tour operators at the same time, so we were amongst crowds walking through the narrow and meandering paths. The sights were unbelievable though.

The curving, winding paths with the bit of sunshine coming through allowed for the texture of the sandstones to be on full display. Slot canyons are also known to be dangerous during rainy season—they're prone to flash floods.

After our last hiking adventures in desert, it was time to backtrack to Flagstaff, where we had stopped by a few days earlier on our way to the Grand Canyon. The airbnb we stayed at had instant access to a light hiking trail right in the backyard. We got our steps in, rewarded ourselves with pizza for dinner, and ended the night with amateur astro photography.

Arizona: More Than Just Desert and Heat

I'll admit that I didn't know much about Arizona beyond its triple digit heat and cacti dominance. Phoenix is a diverse food mecca; Page is a portal to some of the most amazing geological wonders; Flagstaff is far from the desert most people imagine Arizona to be. I was impressed with how socially engaging and culturally connected the community is. There's so much rich history and work being done to preserve these special lands, and I'm glad to have witnessed some of that.
