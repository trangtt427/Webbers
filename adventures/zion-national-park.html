Zion National Park

Utah | May 2023

Explorations into a sandstone-cladded heaven on earth.

A Hiker's Rite of Passage

Beautiful rock formations that are millions of years old, "weeping" canyons, and seasonal rivers—Zion is a red wonderland for nature enthusiasts. I don't hike often, but I've been interested in doing it more because it's the perfect combination of two passions: enjoying the great outdoors and staying active. I've been putting more effort towards a health-conscious lifestyle, even buying a smart watch to monitor and encourage exercise. This Zion trip was a mark of new beginnings; forays into experiences that test my physical limits.

A Great Shuttle System

I was impressed with the shuttles and regulations around personal vehicles. We stayed at a lodge within walking distance to the national park, but there were free shuttle pickups along the main road to bring tourists around. Personal vehicles aren't allowed past a certain point in the national park while these shuttles are in service, telltale signs of a thoughtful tourist infrastructure and active preservation of nature's wonders.

Scenic walks along Zion Park Blvd.

The home of Zion National Park is in Springdale, a small town outfitted with museums, outdoor gear, souvenir shops, countless inns, restaurants, and a bumbleberry pie and ice cream parlor that I regret not visiting more. No matter where you are in Springdale, the canyons will appear massive in any backdrop.

Canyons in someone's backyard.

Friendly Trails for All

Leading up to this trip, I was a bit worried if the trek around the canyons would be too hard for a novice like myself. I didn't have a good benchmark of hike intensities, but I've done Yosemite a few years ago which I thought was difficult. Once we arrived at the park, I appreciated the visibility into the various routes and the national park's recommendations and clear communications of trail difficulties. They had trails for all types of hikers—from casual sightseers to the daring and bold. Over a few days, we did a mixture of intense, moderate, and light hikes.

Our first hike was the Watchman Trail, which is accessible from the visitor center.

Views from the Watchman Trail.

The abundant rain gave life to roaring rivers, thus closing the Narrows during our visit.

Switchbacks en route to Scout Lookout via the West Rim Trail.

High up on the West Rim Trail.

We were within view of Angels Landing and the infamous chained trail that takes hikers up a steep ridge. Permits were required to go up, which we didn't have.

View of Angels Landing from Scout Lookout.

A squirrel friend overlooking the canyons.

The West Rim Trail was an out and back route, so we descended all the way down through the switchbacks and towards the shuttle stop. Next, we did the Riverside Walk and Emerald Pool Trail.

Once water builds up in rocks, it's forced to permeate through walls to create "weeping" canyons.

Waterfalls at the Emerald Pool.

On the last day, we took the car on a scenic drive into the national park and hiked the Canyon Overlook Trail, a one mile hike towards a spellbinding view of the canyons.

The best view of them all.

A Deeper Appreciation for the National Parks System

For someone like me who hasn't done a ton of strenuous hikes, I felt that the park did a good job setting expectations and preparing tourists. The trails were nicely maintained, the transportation system and tons of water refill stations made the place very accessible, so I felt good about putting my money towards keeping all of this sustainable. My takeaway from this trip is to stay active and healthy, so that I can enjoy more of this for as long as possible.
