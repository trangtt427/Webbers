Zion National Park

Utah | May 2023

Explorations into a sandstone-cladded heaven on earth.

A Hiker's Rite of Passage

Beautiful rock formations that are millions of years old, "weeping" canyons, and seasonal rivers—Zion is a red wonderland for nature enthusiasts. I don't hike often, but it's grown on me in the past few years because 1. being outside does wonders for my mental health and 2. staying mobile is really important to me. I've been putting more effort towards a health-conscious lifestyle, even buying a smart watch to monitor stats and encourage exercise. This Zion trip was a mark of new beginnings; going on adventures that test my physical limits.

A Great Shuttle System

My first impression of the park was "wow, this place has it all figured out". Their shuttles and regulations around personal vehicles made the park very accessible. We stayed at a lodge within walking distance to the national park, but there were free shuttles to bring tourists around. Personal vehicles aren't allowed past a certain point in the national park while these shuttles are in service, telltale signs of a thoughtful tourist infrastructure and active preservation of historic lands.

Scenic walks along Zion Park Blvd.

The home of Zion National Park is Springdale, a small town outfitted with museums, outdoor gear, souvenir shops, countless inns, restaurants, and a bumbleberry pie and ice cream parlor that I regret not visiting more. No matter where you are in Springdale, the canyons appear massive in any backdrop.

Canyons in someone's backyard.

Friendly Trails for All

Leading up to this trip, I worried whether the hikes would be too tough. I didn't have a good benchmark of hike intensities, but I've done Yosemite a few years ago which I thought was difficult. Once we arrived at the park, I appreciated the visibility into the various routes and the national park's recommendations and clear communications of trail difficulties. They had trails for all types of hikers—from casual sightseers to the daring and bold. Over a few days, we did a mixture of intense, moderate, and light hikes.

Our first hike was the Watchman Trail, which is accessible from the visitor center.

Views from the Watchman Trail.

The abundant rain gave life to roaring rivers, forcing the Narrows to be closed during our visit.

Switchbacks en route to Scout Lookout via the West Rim Trail.

High up on the West Rim Trail.

We were within view of Angels Landing and the famous chained trail that takes hikers up a steep ridge. Permits were required to go up, which we didn't have (and didn't mind).

View of Angels Landing from Scout Lookout.

A squirrel friend overlooking the canyons.

The West Rim Trail is an out and back trail, so we backtracked all the way down through the switchbacks and towards the shuttle stop. That was our intense hike of the day, but we had some energy left to do the Riverside Walk and Emerald Pool Trail.

Once water builds up in rocks, it's forced to permeate through walls to create "weeping" canyons.

Waterfalls at the Emerald Pool.

On the last day, we took the car on a scenic drive into the national park and hiked the Canyon Overlook Trail, a one mile hike towards a spellbinding view of the canyons.

The best view of them all.

A Deeper Appreciation for the National Parks System

For someone who's a novice, I felt that the park did a good job setting expectations and welcoming people of all levels. The trails were nicely maintained, there were tons of water refill stations, and transportation was reliable. I felt good about putting money towards keeping all of this sustainable. I ended the trip feeling re-invigorated and motivated to prioritize staying active and healthy so that I can enjoy more of this for as long as possible.
